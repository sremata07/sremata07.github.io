## AI is a Dangerous, but Useful Tool

AI is everywhere now, and it has overtaken campuses all around the world. Generative AI can generate something for anyone with a simple prompt and an objective. So when it comes to software engineering, giving an AI a prompt such as "create a web app that does blah blah blah" will result in the AI spitting out a bunch of code that may or may not do what you asked of it. This kind of reliance on AI to complete everything for you is dangerous, as I'm sure many people know at this point that this can negatively affect your critical thinking and your learning. So, have I used AI? Yes, I have. 

## But it's dangerous

AI is a tool, a very fascinating one at that. Like many tools, and many things in life, too much of one thing becomes bad. Since AI's initial boom into the main stream, I have been obversing it, its effects, its uses, and its flaws. With this knowledge, I think that I know how you effectively use AI. So, what kind of tool is AI? I believe AI is a tool that you can use akin to a Pinterest board. You can run code through it, ask it to debug it, see what it changes then test your own knowledge against it. By looking at what the AI does with your issue, you might be able to gather ideas based on what its done that you can apply to your own work. For example, during a couple WODs I have asked AI after a first attempt to check my code as a sort of quality assurance, as I would like to be able to get immediate feedback on what I possibly did wrong. Sometimes it would give me "corrected" code back, and most of those times the corrected code was still slightly wrong, but that doesn't mean I wasn't able to learn from it. By comparing what the AI gave me with what I could see in the WOD walkthroughs, I can deduce and figure out where I went wrong, what I need to work on, and what works and what doesn't. This I believe is an effective, good, and safe way to use AI, as a secondary or tertiary source of information, never as your primary soure.

Now addressing my use of AI for each course element in details:
1. Experience WODS - Yes I have used AI, one called Deepseek, and used to debug my submitted code so that I can compare it to what was given in the WOD walkthrough. 
2. In-class pracice WODS - No, I have not used AI. WODs are best experienced blind, and though the struggle is tough it is necessary. 
3. In-class WODs - No, for the same reason as for in-class practice WODs. However, it is more important to not for these WODs, as they are akin to tests, and testing your knowledge and using AI at the same time is counter-productive.
4. Essays - No, as I know what I want to write more than an AI knows what I want to write, it would only be a waste of time.
5. Final project - Yes, as there are some issues encountered that were not covered in class. I would first try to tackle it on my own, and if I can't figure it out, I would ask AI, Deepseek, to help me figure it out. Then, I would try to work it out using it's guidance, but never use exactly what it gave me. Sources such as React's own guides to its framework was where I would ultimately turn to when tackling issues.
6. Learning a concept/tutorial - Yes, but only for one specific assignment. I couldn't figure out how to install PostgreSQL and I asked an AI, Deepseek, to help me install the program. Once successfully installed, I was able to follow the instructions given without AI-assistance.
7. Answering a question in class or in Discord - No, as whenever I had issues I would look through #smart_questions I would find an answer relatively easily.
8. Asking or answering a smart question - No, as even if I had a smart question or had an answer to one, I wouldn't need AI to do that.
9. Coding example - Yes I would have AI, Deepseek, give me an example on how to use certain functions or elements that I couldn't figure out.
10. Explaining code - Yes I would have AI, Deepseek, go over some of my code and debug it and explain to me what I could improve and what I could work on. However, I would only do this if I had an already correct solution given to me as I wouldn't want to take something that is "correct" but was actually incorrect.
11. Writing code - No, I believe writing code and working it out yourself is important, even if you end up creating something slow and inoptimal. 
12. Documenting code - No, because I find documenting code helps me udnerstand what I've written better. It's sort of like double checking your work for me. 
13. Quality assurance - Yes, I would AI, Deepseek, check over my code. But of course, I would only do this if I have a given solution, as I don't want to accidentally build bad coding habits. 
14. Other uses in ICS 314 not listed - No, there are no other instances not listed that I have used AI for ICS 314. 

## Overall, a slightly positive impact

Personally, I think that AI has had a relatively positive impact on my learning. It was helpful when it needed to be helpful, but nothing more nor nothing less. Solid sources of information, such as WOD workthroughs, or detailed syntax and semantics guides, were much more useful for learning. The reason why AI can not be more helpful is because of it's unreliability. AI must fulfill your request, even if that means hallucinating information. This is a common issue among all generative AI, and thus its help must always be taken with a grain of salt. Like I mentioned before, as a secondary or tertiary source of information, AI can really shine as you can fact check what its saying and be able to filter out the junk it spits out and gather what's actually useful. 

## The sharp edge

The main issue with AI is its tendency to hallucinate. I have regrettably found myself stuck, trying to get an AI to unnecessarily correct a mistake its made instead of moving on. This is a waste of time on par with doom scrolling in my opinion. I think education on the dangers on AI is important in education due to its easy access, especially in a course such as software engineering, where safety and private information is at stakes if people are to take this career path.

## Man vs. Machine

The biggest advantage AI has is speed and ease of access. But looking at the classic story of the tortoise vs. the hare, speed isn't always key. While an AI is easier to reach and faster to respond over an educator, an educator knows exactly how they want to teach, and should know exactly how to help you. Another way to look at this is that AI is a jack of all trades master of none, while an educator is the master of one, with the "one" being the subject they're teaching. Thus, the educator will always be a more reliable source over the AI. 

## AI is inevitable

The future will hold AI, whether we like it or not. It's many pros and cons ultimately cancel out in my opinion, if we ignore its crazy environmental impact but that is not important to this subject matter. There will be students who effectively use AI in software engineering, and there will be those who misuse it, and this may ultimately make the field be filled with more dangerous mindsets. But, nothing is perfect, AI, software engineering, humans, nothing can or ever will be perfect. What we can do is educate, learn, and adapt. AI will negatively affect some students, so it is important to teach and thoroughly explain the pros and cons of AI. Have students try to do a previous assignment purely with AI, and have them go through and debug the code and report their findings. Showcase where it can be useful, and where it can be detrimental, then highlight the challenges they will ultimately face when utilizing AI. That way, the damages won't be completely negated but minimized. Then, in the future hopefully this kind of careful mindset will stick, and software engineering education will adapt and overcome.

## In conclusion,

AI is a tool. Whether it's used or misused is up to the user, so ultimately it's down for software engineering courses to implement it into their education. I think a truly good way to do this is to have students complete some sort of assignment, and then have them recreate the assignment purely through AI. That way, after learning how to do the assignment, they can see how AI tackles it. Then, they will most likely be able to see where it excels and also where it stumbles. Hands on experience is the best way to learn, in my opinion, and software engineering is all about puts your fingers on the keyboard and working it out. Nevertheless, the future is unsure and the only certainty is the progression of AI. So caution is advised, and here's to hoping everything will work out in the end.
